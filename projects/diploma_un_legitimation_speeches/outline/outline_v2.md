## Оглавление

**Introduction** (~8%, ~7 200 зн.) `→ chapters/00_introduction.md`

1. Relevance and Scientific Significance
   — Norm contestation intensifying; UNGA as key legitimation arena in absence of formal adjudication
   — Gap: no systematic corpus-based comparative mapping of legitimation strategies across full UNGDC
   — Link to IL erosion and communicative power debates

2. Research Questions and Hypotheses
   — Main RQ: What legitimation strategies are employed by state leaders in UNGA General Debate speeches to justify conduct alleged to violate international law (1946–2023)?
   — Sub-Q1: Which strategies are most frequently used?
   — Sub-Q2: How has the distribution of strategies evolved across historical periods?
   — Sub-Q3: Do states from different geopolitical blocs draw on distinguishable legitimation repertoires, or does discourse converge across bloc boundaries?
   — **H1 (convergence):** Post-9/11 counter-terrorism framing increases uniformly across all blocs with no significant inter-bloc differentiation. Falsifiable if one bloc shows systematically greater uptake.
   — **H2 (repertoire):** Dominant strategies function as normative-legal action (Habermas) — Charter invocations, sovereignty appeals — not as purely strategic evasion or denial.
   — **H3 (temporal):** Strategy distribution shifts across historical periods; counter-terrorism dominant post-2001. Falsifiable if distribution is stable.
   — **H4 (geopolitical):** Western states show systematically lower normative-legal framing than Soviet/Eastern and Non-Aligned states.

3. Research Goal and Objectives (Objectives 1–5)

4. Scope and Limitations
   — UNGA General Debate only (not UNSC); legitimation discourse not legality; translation constraints

5. Structure of the Thesis

**2. Theoretical Framework** (~15%, ~13 500 зн.) `→ chapters/02_theoretical_framework.md`

2.1. Habermas's Theory of Communicative Action: Core Tenets and Typology
   — Four types of social action: teleological (strategic) / normatively regulated / dramaturgical / communicative
   — Validity claims: truth (*Verständigung*), rightness (*Richtigkeit*), sincerity (*Wahrhaftigkeit*)
   — Communicative vs. strategic action as the central analytical distinction

2.2. Communicative Action in International Relations
   — Risse (2000): logic of arguing vs. bargaining in world politics
   — Müller (2004): arguing/bargaining as speech acts vs. communicative/strategic as orientations
   — Bjola (2005): deliberative legitimacy; Kosovo and Iraq case studies
   — Head (2008): critical theory and humanitarian intervention

2.3. Operationalizing Legitimation in Diplomatic Discourse
   — Two analytical dimensions: (1) type of alleged violation; (2) legitimation strategy
   — Nine communicative roles mapped to four Habermas types (table)
   — Eight manual legitimation strategy categories

2.4. Research Hypotheses (formal statement, H1–H4)

**3. Methodology** (~13%, ~11 700 зн.) `→ chapters/03_methodology.md`

3.1. Research Design: A Two-Level Analytical Strategy
   — **Level 1 — Corpus-level keyword signal analysis:** full UNGDC, 7 thematic signals, 9 sub-periods, disaggregated by Cold War bloc; basis for H1 and H3 corpus-level tests
   — **Level 2 — Directed qualitative content analysis:** 33 case-years, Habermas typology classification, basis for H2–H4
   — Two-stage procedure within Level 2: descriptive mapping → comparative qualitative interpretation
   — Unit of analysis: argumentative segment (rhetorically complete justificatory move)

3.2. Data: The Harvard UNGDC
   — Baturo et al. (2017); Jankin et al. (2025): 11,127 speeches, 198 countries, 1946–2023
   — Genre stability; strategic self-presentation as primary analytical object
   — Python-assisted pre-processing; translation and OCR limitations

3.3. Case Identification and Geopolitical Classification
   — Criterion-based exhaustive selection: 33 state-years, 18 states, 4 periods
   — Case identification via UN reporting, NGO documentation, academic secondary literature
   — Cold War bloc classification (Western / Soviet-Eastern / Non-Aligned Movement):
     · Rationale: 77-year scope; majority (1946–1989) is bipolar system; bloc = shared normative context
     · BLOC_TRANSITIONS: 14 Eastern European states reclassified on NATO accession date

3.4. Coding Framework
   — **3.4.1** Habermas typology as master coding architecture: 9 roles → 4 types table; theoretical grounding in Habermas (1984), Risse (2000), Müller (2004), Bjola (2005)
   — **3.4.2** Keyword-based classification procedure: score = keyword hits / 1000 words per lexicon; dominant role = max non-zero score; unclassified = all-zero
   — **3.4.3** Historical periodization: Cold War (1946–1989) / Post–Cold War (1990–2001) / Post-9/11 Security Turn (2002–2013) / Post-2014 Normative Fragmentation

3.5. Statistical Methods
   — Chi-square (χ²) + Cramér's V: independence tests for Habermas types × bloc / × period
   — Fisher's exact test: pairwise bloc comparisons (small N, expected frequencies < 5)
   — Adjusted standardized residuals (Haberman, 1973): cell-level deviation identification
   — Correspondence analysis (CA via SVD): geometric representation of role-bloc/period associations
   — One-way ANOVA: inter-bloc signal differences pre- vs. post-2001 (H1 corpus test)
   — Two-sample t-test (independent samples): pre/post-2001 within-bloc shift

3.6. Reliability, Validity, and Scope of Claims
   — Double-coding ~20%; Cohen's κ ≥ 0.70 target; audit trail
   — Construct validity: theoretical derivation from Habermas + IR applications
   — Scope: within-sample patterns; no legal adjudication; UNGA only; translation constraints

4.5. Temporal Dynamics: Strategy Evolution and the H3 / H1 Assessment
   — Cold War: normative dominates (63.6%) → Post-9/11: communicative surges (71.0%)
   — χ²(6) = 21.06, p = 0.0018** → H3 supported
   — Counter-terrorism: 5 → 19 → 11 across periods

4.6. Geopolitical Bloc Differentiation: The H4 Assessment
   — χ²(4) = 11.99, p = 0.017*; Fisher's exact: Western vs. Soviet normative OR=0.20 p=0.023*, Western vs. NAM normative OR=0.14 p=0.003**
   — Correspondence analysis: Dim1 = 82.5% inertia; Western at justification pole, Soviet/NAM at normative pole
   — H4 supported; interpretation of Western bloc's lower normative-legal framing
