# Структура работы — Strategies of Legitimizing Violations of International Law in Leaders' Speeches at the United Nations General Assembly (1945–2023)
_Версия 1, 2026-05-13_

## Оглавление

**Introduction** (~8%, ~7 200 зн.)

1. Relevance and Scientific Significance
   — Practical: norm contestation intensifying; UNGA as key legitimation arena in absence of formal adjudication
   — Scientific: gap in systematic comparative mapping of legitimation strategies across the full UNGDC corpus
   — Link to ongoing debates on IL erosion and communicative power

2. Research Questions and Hypotheses
   — Main RQ: What legitimation strategies are employed by state leaders in UNGA General Debate speeches to justify conduct alleged to violate international law (1945–2023)?
   — Sub-Q1: Which strategies are most frequently used?
   — Sub-Q2: How have strategies evolved across historical periods?
   — Sub-Q3: Do states from different geopolitical blocs draw on distinguishable legitimation repertoires, or does the discourse converge across bloc boundaries?
   — H1 (convergence): Post-9/11 counter-terrorism framing increases uniformly across all geopolitical blocs with no significant bloc-based differentiation — ideational convergence, not divergence. Falsifiable if one bloc shows systematically greater uptake than others.
   — H2 (repertoire): Dominant strategies function as normative-legal action (Habermas) — legal Charter invocations, sovereignty appeals, self-defense claims — not as purely strategic evasion or denial.
   — H3 (temporal): Strategy distribution shifts across historical periods — counter-terrorism dominant post-2001, sovereignty-revisionist framing intensifies post-2014. Falsifiable if distribution is stable across periods.
   — H4 (geopolitical): Western states show systematically lower normative-legal framing relative to Soviet/Eastern and Non-Aligned states — observable despite surface convergence in H1.

3. Research Goal and Objectives
   — Goal: identify and systematize legitimation strategies; trace their evolution
   — Objectives 1–5 (as in CONTEXT.md)

4. Scope and Limitations
   — Corpus limited to UNGA General Debate (not UNSC)
   — Study examines legitimation discourse, not legality
   — Translation and strategic omission as constraints

5. Structure of the Thesis

**2. Theoretical Framework** (~15%, ~13 500 зн.)

2.1. Habermas's Theory of Communicative Action: Core Tenets and Typology
   — Origins: critique of instrumental reason; lifeworld vs. system
   — Typology of social actions: teleological / normatively regulated / dramaturgical / communicative action
   — The most significant type for this study: communicative action — action oriented toward understanding (Verständigung), not strategic success
   — Validity claims: truth, rightness, sincerity
   — Communicative vs. strategic action: the central analytical distinction

2.2. Communicative Action in International Relations
   — Risse (2000): arguing vs. bargaining in world politics
   — Müller (2004): communicative action and IL compliance
   — Bjola (2005): deliberative legitimacy as non-coerced commitment; Kosovo / Iraq case studies
   — Head (2008): critical theory and humanitarian intervention
   — Key synthesis: when do state leaders argue vs. merely perform argument?

2.3. Operationalizing Legitimation in Diplomatic Discourse
   — Legitimation as discursive practice: from Habermas to rhetorical analysis
   — Two analytical dimensions:
     (1) type of alleged violation addressed
     (2) legitimation strategy deployed
   — Coding categories (derived from theory + literature): violation types × strategy types

2.4. Research Hypotheses (formal statement)

**3. Methodology** (~13%, ~11 700 зн.)

3.1. Research Design: Directed Qualitative Content Analysis
   — Rationale for directed (vs. inductive) CA: theory provides initial categories (Hsieh & Shannon, 2005)
   — Supplemented by limited descriptive quantification: code frequencies and co-occurrence (not full QTA)
   — Two-stage analysis:
     (1) Descriptive mapping — frequency and co-occurrence
     (2) Comparative qualitative interpretation — how same strategies manifest across contexts
   — Unit of analysis: sentence / paragraph level, depending on rhetorical completeness (Krippendorff, 2018)

3.2. Corpus and Data: the Harvard UNGDC
   — Baturo et al. (2017): description, validation, prior uses of the corpus
   — Scope: [N TBD] speeches, 1946–2023; machine-readable, standardised texts
   — Period of focus: post–Cold War (~1990–2023)

3.3. Case Identification: Criterion-Based Exhaustive Selection
   — Inclusion criterion: state faced documented allegation of contested conduct AND speech addresses it
   — Case identification: UN reporting, NGO documentation, news archives — contextual indicators, not proof of illegality
   — Sample: all qualifying speeches; no predetermined N; exhaustive within criterion
   — Descriptive dimensions: regional distribution, regime type, violation type coverage, temporal spread
   — Illustrative case types: counter-terrorism operations; sovereignty responses to external critique; conflict-related civilian harm allegations; treaty/UNSC-resolution non-compliance

3.4. Coding Framework
   — Dimension 1 — Violation types (5 categories)
   — Dimension 2 — Legitimation strategies (8 categories)
   — Codebook: definitions, inclusion criteria, decision rules for ambiguous cases
   — Iterative refinement procedure

3.5. Reliability, Validity, and Scope of Claims
   — Inter-coder reliability: double-coding on initial subset; disagreement resolution; audit trail
   — What the study claims vs. what it does not claim (no adjudication of legality)
   — Translation and omission as constraints
