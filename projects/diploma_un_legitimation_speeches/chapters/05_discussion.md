# 5. Discussion

## 5.1. Hypothesis Assessment

Four hypotheses were specified in Chapter 2 and subjected to empirical test in Chapter 4. The results are uneven in instructive ways.

**H1 (ideational convergence)** is supported unambiguously. The post-9/11 adoption of counter-terrorism framing is not a Western or Soviet/Eastern phenomenon — it is a cross-bloc one. ANOVA results for the pre-2001 and post-2001 periods both fail to detect inter-bloc differentiation (F = 0.39, p = .678 and F = 1.29, p = .288 respectively), while within-bloc t-tests confirm that all three blocs show a statistically significant increase in counter-terrorism keyword density after September 2001. The implication is that UNSC Resolution 1373 and the normative infrastructure of the "war on terror" created a legitimation resource that was taken up across the international system — not selectively, but uniformly, by states with widely divergent strategic interests and adversarial relationships. Counter-terrorism framing became, in effect, a shared rhetorical commons.

**H2 (repertoire)** is supported with a significant qualification. The hypothesis predicted that dominant strategies would be normative-legal — Charter invocations, self-defense claims, sovereignty appeals. The data show something more complex. Strategic action is indeed absent as a dominant mode — zero classified segments are coded under denial, counter-accusation, or delegitimation as their primary Habermas type — confirming that states before the General Assembly do not simply refuse to legitimize. But the dominant mode is not normative-legal (36.1 percent) but communicative, specifically justificatory (51.5 percent). Direct invocations of Article 51 are nearly absent from the manual coding (n = 1); the more common pattern is the construction of necessity arguments through counter-terrorism framing, with formal legal citation as support rather than as the primary vehicle. H2 requires reformulation: the repertoire is genuinely legitimation-oriented, but the dominant register is argumentative rather than norm-invoking.

**H3 (temporal shift)** is supported. The chi-square result for Habermas action types across periods — χ²(6) = 21.06, p = .0018, Cramér's V = .33 — is the most statistically robust finding in the analysis. The shift from Cold War normative dominance (63.6 percent normative in the 1946–1989 sample) to post-9/11 communicative dominance (71.0 percent communicative in 2002–2013) represents not a gradual drift but a threshold crossing. The post-2014 partial reversion toward normative framing (normative rising from 16.1 to 25.9 percent) suggests that the sovereignty-revisionist rhetoric of the fragmentation period partially re-activated the legal argumentation register that the War on Terror years had displaced.

**H4 (geopolitical variation)** is supported. Fisher's exact tests establish significant pairwise differentiation between the Western bloc and both the Soviet/Eastern (p = .023) and Non-Aligned (p = .003) blocs on normative action, with the Western bloc showing a pronounced deficit. The correspondence analysis positions justification and the Western bloc at the same pole of the primary dimension, which accounts for 82.5 percent of total inertia — an exceptionally high concentration indicating that the geopolitical axis of differentiation in legitimation registers is essentially one-dimensional. Soviet/Eastern and Non-Aligned blocs are not significantly differentiated from each other on any Habermas type.

## 5.2. Communicative vs. Strategic Action in Practice

The finding that most directly speaks to the Habermasian theoretical framework — and that the prior case-study literature anticipated but could not establish at scale — is the complete absence of strategic action as a dominant communicative mode across three Cold War blocs, four historical periods, and eight distinct legitimation strategy types. Risse (2000) and Bjola (2005) argued theoretically that multilateral forums impose communicative constraints on state speech acts; the corpus-level evidence here provides systematic empirical confirmation of that proposition beyond the individual episodes they examined.

Habermas (1984) distinguishes communicative action from strategic action on the criterion of orientation: communicative action is oriented toward *Verständigung* — reaching understanding through the exchange of reasons — while strategic action is oriented toward causal success, treating the other as a means rather than an interlocutor. The empirical pattern in the UNGA corpus suggests that the General Assembly forum imposes a structural constraint on strategic action at the textual level: every coded speech act, including those that colloquially might be described as deflection or counter-attack, contains sufficient justificatory or normative content to be classified under communicative or normative action. The forum's public character, universal membership, and founding normative architecture appear to require — at minimum — the performance of legitimation.

The qualification matters. Risse (2000) and Müller (2004) both observe that the distinction between sincere argumentation and strategic mimicry of argument is empirically difficult to establish from discourse data alone. A state may invoke self-defense language without any genuine orientation toward shared normative evaluation; the verbal form of legitimation does not guarantee its communicative substance. What the data establish is more modest but not trivial: states before the General Assembly cannot afford to *appear* strategically oriented. The institutional setting generates a performative demand for normative justification that shapes the rhetorical surface of diplomatic discourse regardless of underlying intent. This is consistent with the logic of deliberative legitimacy that Bjola (2005) identifies: the construction of normative consensus through public argumentation operates as a constraint on state behavior even when states have no intention of being bound by the consensus they construct.

The bloc-level register differentiation identified under H4 adds a further layer of theoretical complexity. Western states construct necessity arguments; non-Western states invoke legal norms. Both are forms of legitimation, but they make structurally different validity claims (Habermas, 1984): communicative action claims normative rightness through propositional argument about consequences and proportionality, while normative action claims rightness through the assertion of shared binding standards. The Western preference for justificatory framing over formal legal invocation plausibly reflects the structural position of states whose contested actions have often been undertaken without Security Council authorization — a context in which formal Charter invocations would be legally and politically incoherent. The structural logic is documented in Section 4.6: when the formal Charter framework is unavailable, the substitution is quasi-legal necessity argumentation rather than an abandonment of legitimation. The non-Western preference for normative-legal framing, by contrast, reflects the centrality of Article 2(7) and formal legal equality to states whose primary exposure to international criticism concerns the exercise of domestic sovereign authority rather than external military conduct.

## 5.3. Theoretical Contributions and Limitations

The analysis makes three contributions to the literature on legitimation discourse in international politics.

First, it provides systematic, corpus-based evidence for patterns that prior IR scholarship has described primarily through case analysis. Bjola (2005), Risse (2000), and Müller (2004) establish the relevance of communicative action to diplomatic practice through close readings of individual episodes — Kosovo, Iraq, the Helsinki process. The present analysis shows, across 33 case-years and 102 coded segments, that communicative action is not only theoretically applicable but empirically prevalent — and more prevalent than normative-legal invocation in the post-Cold War period. The shift to a corpus-level perspective does not replace the qualitative depth of case analysis; it provides the horizontal dimension that case studies structurally cannot.

Second, the register differentiation finding contributes to ongoing debates about what the "rules-based order" means in practice. States that rhetorically champion that order invoke formal legal norms less frequently in cases of contested conduct than their non-Western counterparts — a pattern confirmed at the p < .01 level for the Western vs. Non-Aligned comparison. This is not evidence of hypocrisy in any simple sense; it reflects different structural exposures to the Charter architecture. Koskenniemi's (2005) account of the structure of international legal argument is instructive here: legal argumentation oscillates between descending claims (appeals to objective norms) and ascending claims (appeals to state consent and practice), and states whose contested actions fall outside the formal Charter authorization framework face a structural disincentive against descending invocations of Charter law — because those very norms would render their conduct indefensible. The quasi-legal justificatory register characteristic of Western diplomatic argumentation in the corpus is, in this reading, a structurally conditioned adaptation rather than a strategic preference. The empirical pattern — non-Western states invoking Charter provisions and legal instruments more consistently, while Western argumentation operates through necessity claims and securitization framing — complicates narratives in which Western states function as primary custodians of the rules-based order, pointing instead toward structural asymmetries that are observable in the discourse independently of any actor's stated normative commitments.

Third, the temporal finding supports the argument that September 2001 functioned as a discursive turning point — not merely a geopolitical one — that reconfigured the normative vocabulary available to all state actors regardless of strategic alignment. Wiener's (2014) theory of contestation provides the theoretical framing: norm-generative events of sufficient salience can reset the available repertoire of legitimation resources across the international system, such that new discursive resources become available to actors whose prior normative positions would not ordinarily support them. The institutionalization of UNSC Resolution 1373's counter-terrorism framework is precisely such an event: it created a legitimation resource adopted rapidly and uniformly across three blocs with widely divergent strategic interests.

Two limitations constrain the scope of these contributions. The keyword-based classification is necessarily reductive: the nine communicative roles are operationalized through lexicons that capture dominant patterns while missing indirect or highly institutionalized formulations, as the elevated Western unclassified rate (11.1 percent) indicates. This is not a methodological failure — it is an analytically informative asymmetry — but it means the classification performs differentially across rhetorical registers and cannot fully capture the hedged, institutionally framed argumentation characteristic of Anglo-American diplomatic prose. The analysis also covers a single venue. Whether the legitimation patterns identified in General Debate speeches characterize diplomatic discourse more broadly — in the Security Council, in bilateral channels, in official state media — remains an open question that the UNGDC corpus, by design, cannot address.

## 5.4. Directions for Future Research

Three extensions appear most immediately productive. The first is a cross-venue comparison: systematically applying the coding framework to Security Council debates for the same state-years as the present sample would test whether legitimation registers shift when the audience changes from the universal membership of the General Assembly to the restricted great-power veto context of the Council. The expectation, consistent with audience-design principles in discourse analysis [SOURCE NEEDED], is that Security Council rhetoric would show higher normative-legal framing and reduced counter-terrorism argumentation — the Council's institutional architecture incentivizes legal citation more directly than the General Debate format, while the absence of a universal audience reduces the rhetorical payoff of broad-coalition counter-terrorism framing.

The second extension concerns the temporal scope of the corpus itself. Pre-1956 speeches — which fall within the UNGDC's coverage from Jankin, Baturo, and Dasandi (2025) but are underrepresented in the present sample due to sparser documentation of contested episodes — would allow assessment of whether Cold War normative dominance was present from the founding of the UN system or emerged gradually over the first decade of the organization's operation. The hypothesis that early General Assembly sessions showed more explicit legal-formalist argumentation, before counter-terrorism framing and quasi-legal necessity arguments became available, is testable with the extended corpus.

The third extension addresses the translation constraint. All non-English speeches in the UNGDC are represented through official UN translations. For the Arabic, Chinese, and Russian subcorpora — which together account for the majority of the Non-Aligned and Soviet/Eastern segments in the present sample — systematic comparison of original-language texts with their English translations would test whether the keyword-based classification performs equivalently across the two versions or whether the translation process consistently flattens strategic distinctions in ways that distort the role assignments. Such a comparison would be particularly important for the assessment of the H4 finding, where bloc-level differences in normative-legal framing are substantial and theoretically consequential.
