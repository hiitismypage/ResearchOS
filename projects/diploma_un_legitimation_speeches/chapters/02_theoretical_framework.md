# 2. Theoretical Framework

## 2.1. Habermas's Theory of Communicative Action: Core Tenets and Typology

The theoretical foundation of this study rests on Jürgen Habermas's theory of communicative action, developed in *The Theory of Communicative Action*, first published in German in 1981. Habermas's project was animated by dissatisfaction with accounts of rationality that reduced it to instrumental and strategic reason, oriented toward the efficient pursuit of pre-given ends. Against this, he argued that a second form of rationality — *communicative rationality* — is embedded in the structures of language use and oriented not toward success but toward mutual understanding (*Verständigung*). This shift from a subject-object to a subject-subject paradigm is the conceptual foundation on which his typology of social action rests.

In Volume 1, Chapter I, Habermas distinguishes four concepts of social action. *Teleological action* — and, when it incorporates other actors as part of the strategic calculus, *strategic action* — is oriented toward the attainment of an end through the selection of appropriate means. *Normatively regulated action* refers to behavior governed by shared group values; in later institutionalist terms, it approximates what March and Olsen would call a logic of appropriateness. *Dramaturgical action* involves the self-presentation of an actor before an audience, the management of impressions in a social performance. *Communicative action* is the fourth and analytically central type: action coordinated through language oriented toward reaching intersubjective agreement, in which actors seek to harmonize their plans not through incentives, threats, or norm compliance alone, but by advancing claims that are open to reasoned acceptance or rejection. As Habermas puts it, "the communicative model of action does not equate action with communication. Language is a means of communication which serves mutual understanding, whereas actors, in coming to an understanding with one another so as to coordinate their actions, pursue their particular aims" (Habermas, 1984, p. 101).

Following the IR applications of Habermas developed by Risse and Bjola, this study focuses on three validity claims most relevant to political justification. *Truth* concerns whether the propositional content corresponds to states of affairs in the objective world. *Rightness* concerns whether the utterance is normatively appropriate within a shared social world and whether the underlying norm can be justified. *Sincerity* concerns whether the expression corresponds to the speaker's actual intentions. These claims carry the expectation that they could, in principle, be defended discursively if challenged. The communicative model treats speech not as the mere transmission of information but as a form of social action in which coordination depends on the intersubjective recognition of validity.

The distinction between communicative and strategic action is the analytical axis most relevant to this study. Strategic action is oriented toward success (*Erfolg*) — the achievement of the actor's own objectives regardless of others' genuine agreement; communicative action is oriented toward understanding (*Verständigung*) — a shared assessment of a situation arrived at through reasons that all parties can in principle accept. Habermas identifies a degenerate but empirically pervasive phenomenon he terms *systematically distorted communication*: strategic action dressed in the grammar of communicative exchange, deploying the surface forms of reasoned argument while remaining, at the level of orientation, purely strategic. This tension is especially relevant for UNGA speeches, where heads of government may justify legally contested conduct before a universal audience in the language of reasoned argument, even when the purpose is reputational management rather than genuine argumentative revision.

## 2.2. Communicative Action in International Relations

The application of Habermas's theory to world politics required a deliberate theoretical translation. Much of mainstream IR theory, particularly rationalist and realist work, had long been organized around interest-based models — rational choice bargaining, deterrence theory, the strategic logic of great-power competition — that treated communication as epiphenomenal to power and interest. The constructivist turn of the 1990s brought normative factors back into analytical view, but the specific question of argumentation as a mode of social action required a further step.

Thomas Risse (2000) made that step in a widely cited contribution. Against the bilateral opposition between consequentialist rationalism and constructivist appropriateness, Risse proposed a third mode of social interaction: the *logic of arguing*. "Processes of argumentation, deliberation, and persuasion," he contended, "constitute a distinct mode of social interaction to be differentiated from both strategic bargaining...and rule-guided behavior" (Risse, 2000, p. 1). Actors engaged in arguing advance truth claims or normative validity claims with a genuine openness to revising their positions in light of better arguments — which is what distinguishes arguing from bargaining (adjusting positions through promises and threats) and from *rhetorical action* (deploying the forms of argument without readiness for revision) (Risse, 2000). Risse identified two distinct contexts in which argumentation functions in world politics: closed, small-group diplomatic negotiations where genuine preference change is at least conceivable; and open, public discourses addressed to wider audiences where argumentative accountability operates at a different scale. The UNGA General Debate belongs to the second category — its institutionalized, universally public character makes it a site where states must advance normative justifications subject, in principle, to scrutiny from all 193 member states. Whether that scrutiny produces genuine arguing or merely a strategic performance of argument is precisely the empirical question this study examines.

Harald Müller (2004) introduced an important conceptual refinement to Risse's framework. Risse had treated "arguing" and "communicative action" as roughly synonymous — both contrasted with bargaining and strategic action. Müller showed that this collapsed a critical distinction: "arguing and bargaining are simply two different types of speech acts... Communicative and strategic action, in contrast, are not speech acts, but types of action that include the wider orientation of the speakers" (Müller, 2004, p. 396). The consequence is that strategic actors can and do use arguing speech acts, deploying the form of reasoned justification instrumentally without any genuine openness to argumentative revision. As Müller observes, "Jon Elster (1991) has shown how strategic actors would use arguing for winning over their partners to their own preferences if they believe that arguments are a better way to win than promises and threats" (Müller, 2004, p. 397). For this study, the distinction is analytically essential: the presence of arguing speech acts in UNGA addresses does not establish that the speaker is engaged in communicative action. The coding framework, developed in Section 2.3 below, therefore tracks strategies — types of speech act — separately from the question of orientation, which is addressed in the interpretive analysis of Chapter 4.

Corneliu Bjola (2005) offered the most direct bridge between the Habermasian framework and the analysis of legitimation in diplomatic practice. Bjola argued that legal compliance criteria — specifically, UNSC authorization — are insufficient to distinguish legitimate from illegitimate uses of force, because legal standards can themselves become objects of strategic manipulation. He proposed instead the concept of *deliberative legitimacy*, defined as "the non-coerced commitment of an actor to obey a norm adopted on the basis of the criteria and rules reached through a process of communicative action" (Bjola, 2005, p. 266). The operative question is whether a discourse is genuinely oriented toward building a reasoned consensus or whether it performs the grammar of argument while remaining structurally closed to outcome: "Do the promoters of military interventions try to reach a reasoned consensus on the need for the use of force, or do they just engage in power games based on credible threats, intimidation or rhetorical exchanges with no visible intention to achieve argumentative consensus?" (Bjola, 2005, p. 267). Applying this distinction to the Kosovo and Iraq cases, Bjola found that NATO's deliberative process prior to 1999 exhibited at least some features of communicative action — positions shifted in response to arguments advanced within the alliance — while the Bush administration's management of the Security Council process in 2002–2003 was characterized by strategic action: legal language (Resolution 1441) used to construct cover for a decision that was not open to argumentative revision (Bjola, 2005).

Naomi Head (2008) extended this analysis toward the procedural conditions under which legitimating discourse can be considered genuinely communicative. Drawing on Habermas's concept of the *ideal speech situation* — defined by equal standing to initiate and participate in discourse, the right to challenge claims, and the absence of coercion — Head identified patterns of structural exclusion that distort apparently deliberative processes even when they occur in formally multilateral settings. "Habermas develops the ideal speech situation in order to be able to distinguish genuine communication from false or pseudo-communication," she observes (Head, 2008, p. 152). In the Kosovo case, the exclusion of Kosovo Albanians from Security Council deliberations and the coercive framing of the Rambouillet negotiations meant that even the procedural forms of dialogue failed to satisfy minimal conditions of non-distorted communication (Head, 2008). For this study, Head's analysis points toward a structural feature of UNGA speeches that bears on their interpretation: states delivering legitimation addresses before the General Assembly are not participants in an equal deliberative exchange — they occupy a structurally marked position within an asymmetric communicative space, defending conduct that is already the subject of public contestation.

The synthesis these four contributions enable can be stated as follows. State leaders addressing the UNGA in the wake of legally contested actions face a situation in which the validity claim of *rightness* — the normative appropriateness of their conduct — is under explicit challenge. They must respond publicly, before a universal audience, and in a genre that carries the institutional expectation of normative justification. The theoretical expectation — grounded in Habermas's typology, Risse's distinction between arguing and rhetorical action, Müller's speech-act correction, and Bjola's deliberative legitimacy framework — is that the dominant mode will be strategic action in communicative form: arguing speech acts deployed without genuine openness to argumentative revision. Verifying that expectation, and mapping the specific strategies through which it is realized, is the task of Chapters 3 and 4.

## 2.3. Operationalizing Legitimation in Diplomatic Discourse

Converting the theoretical framework into an analytical instrument requires specifying what is to be observed in UNGA speech texts. Legitimation, as used in this study, refers to the public construction of normative justifications for conduct whose legality or normative acceptability is publicly disputed. This definition draws on Gronau and Schmidtke's (2016) characterization of legitimation as a goal-oriented practice and connects it to the Habermasian account of validity claims — specifically the claim of *rightness* — as the dimension of speech at stake when states seek to defend contested actions before international audiences.

The coding framework operationalizes legitimation along two analytical dimensions. The first captures the *type of alleged violation* that the speech is responding to: (1) use of force and *jus ad bellum* allegations (contested resort to armed force); (2) IHL allegations (conduct alleged to violate the laws of armed conflict); (3) human rights allegations (domestic repression, mass atrocities, persecution); (4) territorial integrity and sovereignty claims (annexation, occupation, contested secession); (5) treaty and UNSC-resolution non-compliance. These categories are not mutually exclusive — a single address may respond to overlapping allegations — but they provide the primary contextual variable against which the choice of legitimation strategy can be assessed.

The second dimension captures the *legitimation strategy* itself: the discursive move through which the speaker constructs normative cover for the contested action. Eight strategy types are distinguished: (1) *self-defense and necessity claims* — a cluster of defensive justifications encompassing both the legal right of self-defense under Art. 51 of the UN Charter and broader appeals to necessity (security, political, or humanitarian) that frame the action as unavoidable given the circumstances; (2) *sovereignty and non-interference* — characterization of external critique as illegitimate intervention in domestic affairs; (3) *denial and reinterpretation* — factual denial of the alleged conduct or its redescription under a non-violating frame; (4) *moral and humanitarian framing* — positioning the action as morally obligatory or as the lesser harm, including humanitarian justifications that invoke the protection of civilians or endangered populations; (5) *counter-terrorism and securitization framing* — invocation of exceptional threat, terrorist designation, or civilizational danger to justify departure from ordinary legal standards; (6) *whataboutism and tu quoque* — deflection through accusation of the accuser or generalization of the alleged violation as universal; (7) *procedural-legal claims* — appeals to formal procedural compliance, treaty authority, or multilateral authorization; (8) *historical and existential narratives* — justification grounded in historical grievance, territorial-historical claims, or identity-based existential framing.

The cross-tabulation of these two dimensions — violation type against strategy type — generates the analytical matrix for the empirical chapter. Descriptive frequencies identify which strategies are most common within the selected sample and which cluster around particular violation types, providing a basis for assessing the two hypotheses stated in Section 2.4. The qualitative comparative analysis in Chapter 4 then examines how the same strategies manifest differently across actors, periods, and normative contexts, and what that variation reveals about the communicative structure of legitimation at the UNGA.

## 2.4. Research Hypotheses

On the basis of the theoretical framework developed in Sections 2.1–2.3 and the literature reviewed in Chapter 1, four hypotheses are advanced for empirical testing in Chapter 4.

**H1 (ideational convergence).** Post-9/11 counter-terrorism framing increases uniformly across all geopolitical blocs with no statistically significant inter-bloc differentiation — a pattern consistent with ideational convergence rather than divergence. The theoretical basis is twofold. First, UNSC Resolution 1373 (2001) formally established counter-terrorism as a universal obligation, making securitization framing available to all state actors regardless of their underlying normative position. Second, Wiener's (2014) theory of contestation predicts that a norm-generative event of sufficient salience — here, the institutionalization of the counter-terrorism framework — can reset the available repertoire of legitimation resources across the international system, displacing prior bloc-specific patterns. This hypothesis is falsifiable: it would be rejected if one bloc showed systematically greater uptake of counter-terrorism language than others in the post-2001 period.

**H2 (repertoire).** The dominant legitimation strategies across the corpus will function as normative-legal action in Habermas's (1984) typology — oriented toward shared legal frameworks and validity claims of *rightness* — rather than as purely strategic evasion or denial. The theoretical derivation runs as follows. Bjola (2005) and Müller (2004) establish that states in institutionalized multilateral settings deploy arguing speech acts even when their underlying orientation is strategic; the forum structure generates a normative performance requirement. In the UNGA's institutional setting — public, universal, founded on the Charter architecture — states face strong incentives to construct justifications grounded in shared legal standards, because purely strategic speech acts (denial, counter-accusation, delegitimation) forfeit the legitimating function that public justification is meant to serve. The hypothesis is falsifiable: it would be rejected if strategic action — as defined by the Habermas typology — constituted the dominant mode across the sample.

**H3 (temporal shift).** The distribution of legitimation strategies shifts significantly across historical periods, rather than remaining stable across the 77-year period of analysis. Specifically: normative-legal framing is predicted to dominate during the Cold War; communicative justification — particularly counter-terrorism framing — to surge in the post-9/11 period; and sovereignty-revisionist normative framing to intensify after 2014, consistent with the fragmentation of the post-Cold War normative consensus documented in recent IR scholarship. The theoretical basis is Wiener's (2014) norm contestation framework: if norms are continuously contested and their meaning is reproduced through practice, then major ruptures in the normative environment — the end of the Cold War, the post-9/11 security turn, the post-2014 fragmentation — should produce observable shifts in the repertoire of legitimation resources that states can plausibly deploy. The hypothesis is falsifiable: it would be rejected if the chi-square test across periods yielded a non-significant result.

**H4 (geopolitical differentiation).** Legitimation strategies differ systematically across Cold War blocs, with Western states showing a lower reliance on normative-legal framing relative to Soviet/Eastern and Non-Aligned states — a divergence observable despite the surface convergence captured in H1. The theoretical derivation follows from the structural account in Section 2.3: if legitimation strategies are shaped by the normative standards that a speaker's core audience accepts as legitimate grounds, then bloc membership — as a proxy for shared normative context — should predict rhetorical patterns. Western states operating outside the Security Council authorization framework face a structural disincentive against formal Charter invocations; Soviet/Eastern and Non-Aligned states, whose contested conduct typically concerns the exercise of domestic sovereign authority, have stronger incentives to invoke Charter provisions on sovereignty and non-interference. This structural asymmetry — not strategic preference — is predicted to generate the observable register divergence. The hypothesis is falsifiable: it would be rejected if the chi-square and Fisher's exact tests across blocs yielded non-significant results.

All four hypotheses are tested in Chapter 4 against the 33-case sample. Their results are interpreted in Chapter 5.
