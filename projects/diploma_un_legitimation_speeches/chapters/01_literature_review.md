# 1. Literature Review

## 1.1. Legitimacy in International Relations: Theoretical Landscape

Legitimacy has become an increasingly important, if contested, concept in IR scholarship on the postwar international order. Its theoretical status shifted substantially over the past three decades, moving from a largely normative concern — whether particular norms or institutions *deserved* compliance — toward a more empirical and relational question of how legitimacy functions as a social fact structuring state behavior.

Thomas Franck's (1990) foundational contribution established the link between legitimacy and rule compliance within the international legal system. Franck's central question — why states comply with international legal rules in the absence of centralized coercive enforcement — led him to the concept of *compliance pull*: the force that legitimate rules exert on state behavior independently of coercive sanctions. He identified four properties that determine the strength of that pull: determinacy (the clarity and precision of the rule's formulation), symbolic validation (the ritual and procedural markers that authenticate the norm), coherence (its internal consistency with broader legal principles), and adherence (its integration into a recognized normative hierarchy). The framework treated legitimacy as an observable quality of rules rather than a purely philosophical attribute, and positioned it as a matter of degree rather than a binary judgment. This insight provides a starting point for analysing why states seek to attach contested conduct to legal norms that retain recognized legitimacy.

Hurd (1999) offered an equally important reorientation. Departing from coercion- and interest-based accounts of norm compliance, Hurd argued that legitimacy operates as a third mechanism of social control — alongside coercion and self-interest — grounded in internalized belief in the rightness of norms. [SOURCE NEEDED: Hurd, 1999] The critical move was to treat legitimacy as a social fact rather than an evaluative standard. What matters analytically is not whether a norm meets an external normative standard but whether relevant actors believe it to be legitimate. This constructivist reframing opened legitimacy to empirical analysis: if it is constituted through belief and practice, it can be traced in the public statements and justificatory moves of state actors — precisely the objects this study examines.

Clark (2003) and Steffek (2003) extended the analysis toward questions of authority and governance in international society. [SOURCE NEEDED: Clark, 2003; Steffek, 2003] Clark emphasized the role of intersubjective recognition and consensus in conferring authority within international society. Steffek developed a discourse-centered account in which legitimacy depends on public justification and discursive acceptance — a connection that anticipates the analytical move of the present study.

Gronau and Schmidtke (2016) positioned legitimation as an active, top-down practice rather than a passive outcome of norm compliance. Building on Weberian ideas about legitimacy and domination, they developed a typology of legitimation strategies organized around three distinct constituencies: member states, institutional staff, and wider publics. While their empirical focus was on international institutions — the G8 and the IMF during periods of contested authority — their conceptual architecture can be adapted to state actors addressing multilateral audiences.

Wiener's (2018) norm contestation framework provides a further theoretical resource. [SOURCE NEEDED: Wiener, 2018 — key pages on contestation as ongoing practice] Where earlier accounts treated legitimacy as a relatively stable attribute of particular rules, Wiener argues that the meaning and validity of international norms remain subject to continuous contestation in international practice. Legitimation, in this view, is not a one-off act of compliance or defection but a running negotiation over what a norm means and who it binds. This has direct implications for the temporal scope of the present study: the evolution of legitimation strategies from 1946 to 2023 reflects an ongoing contestation over the terms of the postwar normative architecture, not a fixed repertoire deployed against a fixed standard.

## 1.2. Diplomacy as Public Communication: the UNGA as a Rhetorical Arena

The United Nations General Assembly General Debate is one of the most regularized and visible genres of public diplomatic communication in the international system. Convened annually each September, it provides every member state with a designated speaking slot before the full plenary — an opportunity that governments consistently use to state positions on contested norms, respond to international criticism, and present their preferred interpretation of international events before a universal audience. The ritual character of the event — fixed institutional setting, established speaking order, universal participation, near-identical format from year to year — makes it unusually tractable for systematic analysis. Genre constraints are relatively uniform across decades and states, which reduces the interpretive noise that arises when comparing speech events from radically different institutional contexts.

The Harvard UN General Debate Corpus (UNGDC), introduced by Baturo, Dasandi, and Mikhaylov (2017), made systematic text-as-data analysis of the General Debate feasible for the first time. The original dataset comprises 7,314 statements from 198 countries covering sessions 1970–2014; the corpus has since been extended on Harvard Dataverse to cover sessions from 1946 to 2023, comprising 11,127 speeches in its current release. Baturo et al. (2017) demonstrate that GD speeches can serve as empirical proxies for state preferences precisely because they are less constrained than UNGA roll-call votes: unlike voting, where aid relationships and strategic blocs shape behaviour, states deliver their addresses without direct institutional stakes, which means the choice of topics and emphases more freely reflects national priorities. Representatives of the Finnish and German missions to the UN, interviewed by the authors, described GD speeches as the place where states "speak their mind" and "the most sovereign thing that a country does as a member of the UN" (Baturo et al., 2017, p. 2). The authors validate their preference estimates against independent foreign policy measures, demonstrating convergent validity. Prior applications of the dataset have focused on extracting ideological signals and preference positions; the present study approaches the same material with a qualitatively oriented analytical framework applied to a theoretically motivated sub-sample. A relevant limitation is that GD speeches are strategically constructed performances: as Baturo et al. (2017, p. 3) note, "member states present themselves exclusively in the guise in which they wish to be known." For the purposes of the present analysis, however, this is a feature rather than a flaw — the strategic construction of public justification is precisely the object of inquiry.

The General Debate occupies an ambiguous position between communicative and strategic action — a distinction central to the theoretical framework developed in Chapter 2. On one account, it functions as a site of public deliberation: governments advance positions, respond to challenges, and expose their reasoning to scrutiny before a universal and formally equal audience. On another, it operates as a performance arena where states manage reputational risks, address domestic audiences through international ones, and deploy available normative vocabulary without genuine openness to argumentative revision. [SOURCE NEEDED: cite relevant literature on performativity in multilateral diplomacy / public diplomacy] Neither account fully captures the phenomenon. What can be said is that the General Debate is the most consistently attended, most formally institutionalized, and most universally accessible forum in which state leaders publicly articulate legal and normative positions — which is what makes it the appropriate site for analyzing legitimation strategies at the population level rather than one episode at a time.

## 1.3. Existing Research on Legitimation Discourse in UN Speeches: Contributions and Gaps

The scholarly literature on the legitimation of contested conduct in multilateral settings has generated a body of analytically rich case studies that establish the importance of the phenomenon while leaving systematic questions unanswered.

Bjola (2005) provides the most theoretically developed account within this tradition. Applying Habermas's theory of communicative action to NATO's intervention in Kosovo (1999) and the US-led invasion of Iraq (2003), Bjola argues that the legitimacy of military action cannot be settled by reference to UN Charter compliance alone. His concept of *deliberative legitimacy*, defined as "the non-coerced commitment of an actor to obey a norm adopted on the basis of the criteria and rules reached through a process of communicative action" (Bjola, 2005, p. 266), introduces a procedural standard that distinguishes genuine normative justification from strategic persuasion. The diagnostic question is whether the promoters of intervention genuinely sought "a reasoned consensus on the need for the use of force" or merely engaged in "power games based on credible threats, intimidation or rhetorical exchanges with no visible intention to achieve argumentative consensus" (Bjola, 2005, p. 267). Assessed against this standard, the two cases yield contrasting findings. In the Kosovo episode, alliance members modified their positions in response to arguments and the process, however imperfect, remained genuinely open to deliberative revision. The Iraq episode was dominated by strategic action: the United States manipulated the legal framing of Resolution 1441 without openness to argumentative revision, seeking agreement rather than understanding. The analytical value of this distinction is considerable; its empirical scope is not. Two cases, both Western-led, selected for their visibility rather than their representativeness, cannot speak to whether similar patterns obtain across the full range of legitimation contexts the postwar system has generated.

Head (2008) extends the Habermasian approach into a more explicitly critical register, demonstrating that patterns of "exclusion, coercion and illegitimacy… not only challenge the claims to legitimacy of the intervention and of the interveners, but demonstrate the critical power of a communicative framework" (Head, 2008, p. 150). The Kosovo episode supplies the evidence: Kosovar Albanians were structurally absent from Security Council deliberations — having no state and therefore no seat — which meant the principal affected party had no standing in a process nominally conducted on its behalf. The Rambouillet negotiations reproduced the same asymmetry, with the Contact Group setting terms while the threat of air strikes functioned as an instrument of pressure rather than persuasion. Head's concern is normative, but the analytical observation generalizes: case-study methods can identify such structural dynamics with precision, but cannot measure their prevalence, frequency, or variation across the diversity of the UN membership and the full postwar period.

Rapp (2022) approaches the problem through an entirely different method. Drawing on declassified archival documents from British and American decision-makers, he traces the internal reasoning behind legal justifications for force in the Suez crisis (1956) and the Grenada military intervention (1983). His central claim is that "in a deeply legalized system actors feel compelled to portray their behaviors as legal, even if these justifications are tenuous or if pursuing them is time and resource consuming" (Rapp, 2022, p. 337). The justificatory preference followed a clear hierarchy: references to codified treaty obligations were preferred over customary international law, and both were preferred over non-legal rationales, because written legal text offers a standard that opponents find harder to dismiss. In the Suez case, British officials sought not the legally strongest argument but the most *palatably* legal one — the formulation most capable of managing anticipated criticism. In the Grenada case, the United States assembled three individually weak grounds into a composite justification — an invitation from the Governor-General, a threat to American citizens, and authorization under the OECS regional treaty — because no single argument was persuasive in isolation. The contribution is significant, establishing that legitimation is a strategic and resource-intensive practice even for preponderant states. The method, however, forecloses analysis of the public dimension: the speeches delivered to the UN General Assembly in these same episodes remain unexamined, and the gap between internal decision-making and public justification is itself an analytically interesting object.

Scott and Ambler (2007) examine the decline in the perceived legitimacy of US foreign policy after the 2003 invasion of Iraq and argue that this decline cannot be explained by illegality alone. Drawing on the theory of International Law as Ideology, they show that US legitimacy was damaged when discrepancies between American legal rhetoric and the realities of the Iraq campaign became increasingly visible. Their analysis supports the premise of the present study: public legal justification matters because failures of justification can affect how legitimate a state's foreign policy appears to international audiences.

Taken together, this body of work establishes several important premises: that the legitimation of legally contested conduct is a systematic practice across a wide range of states and contexts; that the UN General Assembly and the Security Council are the primary arenas for its public expression; and that the gap between formal legal compliance and perceived legitimacy is analytically tractable. What the existing literature does not provide is a systematic account of which legitimation strategies appear in the UNGDC corpus, in what combinations, with what frequency, and how the distribution has shifted across the postwar period. The published research consists of deep vertical borings into particular episodes; the horizontal view — across states, across time, across violation types — remains unavailable. This study addresses that gap.
