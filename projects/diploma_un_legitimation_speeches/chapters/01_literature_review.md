# 1. Literature Review

## 1.1. Legitimacy in International Relations: Theoretical Landscape

The concept of legitimacy has occupied a central, if often contested, place in IR scholarship since the founding of the postwar international order. Its theoretical status shifted substantially over the past three decades, moving from a largely normative concern — whether particular norms or institutions *deserved* compliance — toward a more empirical and relational question of how legitimacy functions as a social fact structuring state behavior.

Thomas Franck's (1990) foundational contribution established the link between legitimacy and rule compliance within the international legal system. [SOURCE NEEDED: Franck, 1990 — specific pages on compliance pull] Franck identified four properties that confer "compliance pull" on international rules — determinacy, symbolic validation, coherence, and adherence to a hierarchical rule-making process — and argued that the relative strength of these properties accounts for variation in states' voluntary compliance. The framework was normative in its aspirations but empirical in its methodology, treating legitimacy as an observable quality of rules rather than a purely philosophical attribute. Crucially for the present study, Franck's approach implied that legitimacy is something states must actively maintain — a resource that can depreciate when the gap between rhetorical commitment and observable conduct becomes visible.

Hurd's (1999) reorientation proved equally influential. Departing from coercion- and interest-based accounts of norm compliance, Hurd argued that legitimacy operates as a third mechanism: states follow rules partly because they have internalized a belief in the rightness of those rules. [SOURCE NEEDED: Hurd, 1999 — core pages on legitimacy as social fact] The critical move was to treat legitimacy as a social fact rather than an evaluative standard. What matters analytically is not whether a norm is "truly" legitimate but whether relevant actors believe it to be. This constructivist reframing opened legitimacy to empirical analysis: if it is constituted through belief and practice, it can be traced in the public statements and justificatory moves of state actors — precisely the objects this study examines.

Clark (2003) and Steffek (2003) extended the analysis toward questions of authority and governance in international society. [SOURCE NEEDED: Clark, 2003; Steffek, 2003] Clark situated legitimacy within the dynamics of international society, arguing that intersubjective recognition by other states is the primary mechanism through which authority is conferred and contested. Steffek developed a discourse-centered account, drawing on Habermas to argue that international governance institutions legitimate themselves through the quality of the justificatory discourse they generate — a connection that anticipates the analytical move of the present study.

Gronau and Schmidtke (2016) introduced an explicitly strategic dimension into the analysis. Their definition of *legitimation strategies* as "goal-oriented activities employed to establish and maintain a reliable basis of diffuse support" (p. 540) positioned legitimation as an active, top-down practice rather than a passive outcome of norm compliance. Drawing on the Weberian observation that every system of domination "attempts to cultivate the belief in its legitimacy" (p. 539, citing Weber 1978), they proposed a typology of legitimation strategies organized around three distinct constituencies: member states, institutional staff, and wider publics. While their empirical focus fell on international institutions — the G8 and the IMF during periods of contested authority — the conceptual architecture transfers directly to state actors before multilateral audiences. Governments facing international scrutiny engage in precisely this kind of calibrated legitimation work, addressing multiple audiences simultaneously through the same public address.

Wiener's (2018) norm contestation framework provides a further theoretical resource. [SOURCE NEEDED: Wiener, 2018 — key pages on contestation as ongoing practice] Where earlier accounts treated legitimacy as a relatively stable attribute of particular rules, Wiener argues that the meaning and validity of international norms remain subject to continuous contestation in international practice. Legitimation, in this view, is not a one-off act of compliance or defection but a running negotiation over what a norm means and who it binds. This has direct implications for the longitudinal dimension of the present study: the evolution of legitimation strategies from 1946 to 2023 reflects an ongoing contestation over the terms of the postwar normative architecture, not a fixed repertoire deployed against a fixed standard. More recently, Ginsburg (2020) has documented how authoritarian states have become adept at co-opting the forms and language of international legal obligation while subverting their substance — treating the bodies and procedures of international law as arenas for legitimation rather than compliance. [VERIFY: Ginsburg, 2020 — confirm exact title, argument, and page references] Whether this constitutes a qualitative shift in legitimation strategy after 2014 is one of the questions the empirical analysis addresses.

## 1.2. Diplomacy as Public Communication: the UNGA as a Rhetorical Arena

The United Nations General Assembly General Debate is one of the most institutionalized genres of public diplomatic communication in the international system. Convened annually each September, it provides every member state with a designated speaking slot before the full plenary — an opportunity that governments consistently use to state positions on contested norms, respond to international criticism, and project preferred identities before a universal audience. The ritual character of the event — fixed institutional setting, established speaking order, universal participation, near-identical format from year to year — makes it unusually tractable for systematic analysis. Genre constraints are relatively uniform across decades and states, which reduces the interpretive noise that arises when comparing speech events from radically different institutional contexts.

The Harvard UN General Debate Corpus (UNGDC), assembled by Baturo, Dasandi, and Mikhaylov (2017), has made corpus-based analysis of the General Debate feasible at scale for the first time. The dataset collects all speeches delivered at the annual General Debate from 1946 to 2023 in machine-readable form — approximately [ВПИСАТЬ: число речей из UNGDC documentation] speeches from [ВПИСАТЬ: число государств] member states — and has been used to study ideological positioning, foreign policy priorities, and discursive change across the UN membership. Baturo et al. (2017) validate the corpus against independent foreign policy measures and demonstrate its reliability as a basis for text-as-data analysis. Prior quantitative applications of the UNGDC have extracted signals about state preferences and alliance patterns at the level of the full corpus; the present study applies a qualitatively oriented analytical framework to a theoretically motivated sub-sample, approaching the same dataset with different questions and different tools.

The General Debate occupies an ambiguous position between communicative and strategic action — a distinction central to the theoretical framework developed in Chapter 2. On one account, it functions as a site of public deliberation: governments advance positions, respond to challenges, and expose their reasoning to scrutiny before a universal and formally equal audience. On another, it operates as a performance arena where states manage reputational risks, address domestic audiences through international ones, and deploy available normative vocabulary without genuine openness to argumentative revision. What can be said is that the General Debate is the most consistently attended, most formally institutionalized, and most universally accessible forum in which state leaders publicly articulate legal and normative positions — which is what makes it the appropriate site for analyzing legitimation strategies at the population level rather than one episode at a time.

## 1.3. Existing Research on Legitimation Discourse in UN Speeches: Contributions and Gaps

The scholarly literature on the legitimation of contested conduct in multilateral settings has generated a body of analytically rich case studies that establish the importance of the phenomenon while leaving systematic questions unanswered.

Bjola (2005) provides the most theoretically developed account within this tradition. Applying Habermas's theory of communicative action to NATO's intervention in Kosovo (1999) and the US-led invasion of Iraq (2003), Bjola argues that the legitimacy of military action cannot be settled by reference to UN Charter compliance alone. His concept of *deliberative legitimacy*, defined as "the non-coerced commitment of an actor to obey a norm adopted on the basis of the criteria and rules reached through a process of communicative action" (p. 270), introduces a procedural standard that distinguishes genuine normative justification from strategic persuasion. The Kosovo episode, in Bjola's reading, approximated deliberative legitimacy more closely: alliance members modified their positions in response to arguments, and the deliberative process, however imperfect, remained genuinely open. The Iraq episode, by contrast, was characterized by strategic action in Habermas's sense — the United States manipulated the legal framing of Resolution 1441 without openness to argumentative revision, seeking agreement rather than understanding. The analytical value of this distinction is considerable; its empirical scope is not. Two cases, both Western-led, selected for their visibility rather than their representativeness, cannot speak to the question of whether similar patterns obtain across the full range of legitimation contexts the postwar system has generated.

Head (2008) extends the Habermasian approach into a more explicitly critical register, using the Kosovo episode to identify structural patterns of exclusion and coercion within nominally deliberative processes. The observation that Kosovar Albanians were absent from Security Council deliberations — having no state and therefore no seat — points to a systematic feature of international legitimation: the procedures designed to legitimate decisions may themselves exclude the parties most directly affected. Head's concern is normative, but the analytical observation generalizes: case-study methods can identify such structural dynamics with precision, but cannot measure their prevalence, frequency, or variation across the diversity of the UN membership and the full postwar period.

Rapp (2022) approaches the problem through an entirely different method. Drawing on declassified archival documents from British and American decision-makers, he traces the internal reasoning behind legal justifications for force in the Suez crisis (1956) and the Grenada invasion (1983). His central finding is that policymakers actively constructed legal justifications, selecting from available normative vocabulary not on the basis of legal strength but of *legal palatability* — the capacity to manage criticism from anticipated audiences. For Grenada, the United States assembled several individually weak legal arguments into a composite justification, since no single ground was persuasive on its own. The contribution is significant, establishing that legitimation is a strategic and resource-intensive practice even for powerful states. The method, however, forecloses analysis of the public dimension: the speeches delivered to the UN General Assembly during these same episodes remain unexamined, and the distance between internal decision-making and public justification is itself an analytically interesting object.

Scott and Ambler (2007) examine the relationship between legality and legitimacy in the context of US foreign policy after the Iraq invasion, arguing that the contested legal status of the intervention contributed materially to the decline of US international standing. [VERIFY: confirm specific argument of Scott & Ambler, 2007 — source card not fully processed] Their analysis suggests that the legality-legitimacy nexus carries observable consequences for a state's diplomatic capital, and by extension that the choice of legitimation strategy is not cost-free. This observation reinforces the theoretical premise of the present study: the investment states make in constructing and sustaining public justifications reflects a real calculus of reputational risk.

Taken together, this body of work establishes several important premises: that the legitimation of legally contested conduct is a systematic practice across a wide range of states and contexts; that the UN General Assembly and the Security Council are the primary arenas for its public expression; and that the gap between formal legal compliance and perceived legitimacy is analytically tractable. What the existing literature does not provide is a systematic account of which legitimation strategies appear in the UNGDC corpus, in what combinations, with what frequency, and how the distribution has shifted across the postwar period. The published research consists of deep vertical borings into particular episodes; the horizontal view — across states, across time, across violation types — remains unavailable. This study addresses that gap.
