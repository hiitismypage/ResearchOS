# Introduction

When a state uses military force in circumstances that other governments characterize as illegal, the diplomatic calendar offers a predictable ritual. At the United Nations General Assembly, its head of state or foreign minister takes the floor and constructs a public justification. The justification may invoke self-defense under Article 51 of the UN Charter, reframe the operation as counter-terrorism, appeal to humanitarian necessity, contest the credibility of the accusing parties, or some combination of these moves. What varies is the specific configuration and the rhetorical register. What does not vary — and this is the central empirical observation motivating this thesis — is that the move is made — publicly, on the record, in the one forum where every state in the international system holds a seat before an audience of 193 governments.

The universality of this practice — observed consistently across states of varying capabilities and ideological orientations — raises a non-trivial question for international relations theory. Even actors commanding preponderant material power have invested considerable diplomatic resources in constructing legal and normative justifications for conduct subjected to international scrutiny. This suggests that legitimacy operates as a structural condition of international politics, not merely as a cosmetic concern. As Hurd (1999) argues, actors comply with or resist norms partly on the basis of whether they perceive those norms as legitimate; the corollary is that states under normative pressure have strong incentives to contest the terms of that pressure publicly. The UN General Debate is the institutional arena where this contestation plays out at the highest level of formality.

The scholarly literature on legitimation in international relations has grown substantially over the past three decades. Theoretical accounts now range from Franck's (1990) early work on the compliance pull of legitimate rules, through Hurd's (1999) social constructivist account of legitimacy as belief, to Bjola's (2005) application of Habermasian communicative action theory to the justification of force, and Wiener's (2018) framework for norm contestation across institutional settings. What this body of work does not provide is a systematic, corpus-based account of which legitimation strategies actually appear in leaders' speeches at the UNGA, in what combinations, and how the repertoire has changed from the founding of the United Nations to the present. The existing empirical literature is built predominantly from case studies of individual episodes — NATO's intervention in Kosovo in 1999 (Bjola, 2005; Head, 2008), the US-led military operation in Iraq in 2003 (Rapp, 2022; Scott & Ambler, 2007), the Suez crisis of 1956 (Rapp, 2022) — each of which yields analytic depth but cannot speak to aggregate patterns, frequency distributions, or longitudinal evolution. This thesis addresses that gap.

Three research questions structure the analysis. The main question asks: *What legitimation strategies are employed by state leaders in speeches delivered at the UN General Assembly General Debate to justify conduct alleged to violate international law, from 1946 to 2023?*[^1] Two sub-questions give the investigation further structure: Which of the identified strategies occur most frequently across the sampled corpus? And how has the distribution of strategies shifted across the period under study, from the Cold War decade through the post-1990 normative expansion to the fragmentation of the rules-based order after 2014?

The study proceeds from two theory-driven hypotheses. The first (H1) holds that public justificatory discourse will cluster around two dominant violation types: contested uses of force — particularly those where the self-defense rationale under Article 51 is invoked under disputed circumstances — and allegations of humanitarian harm or human rights abuse. The second (H2) holds that the dominant strategies will be self-defense and necessity claims, counter-terrorism framings, sovereignty and non-interference appeals, and the rhetorical delegitimation of accusers. Both are treated as theory-informed starting points subject to empirical revision, not as conclusions to be confirmed.

The empirical basis of the analysis is the Harvard UN General Debate Corpus (UNGDC), a machine-readable dataset of all speeches delivered at the General Assembly's annual General Debate from 1946 to 2023 — 11,127 speeches from 198 countries. The analytical sample is identified through a criterion-based procedure: state-years in which the government faced externally documented allegations of legally or normatively contested conduct are identified using UN reporting (General Assembly and Security Council records, special rapporteur reports), NGO documentation (Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch), and academic secondary literature on specific conflicts and episodes. Python scripts assist in extracting and keyword-filtering the corresponding speeches from the full corpus; every identified speech is then read and coded manually. The coding employs directed qualitative content analysis (Hsieh & Shannon, 2005) and proceeds in two stages. In the first stage, descriptive mapping establishes the frequency and co-occurrence of coded strategies across the full sample. In the second stage, comparative qualitative interpretation examines how specific strategies are realized across actors, periods, and normative contexts. The coding framework operates along two analytical dimensions: the type of alleged violation addressed, and the legitimation strategy deployed in response. Both dimensions are derived deductively from existing theory and refined through iterative engagement with the corpus material. The qualitative findings are supplemented by descriptive frequency counts and co-occurrence mapping across the full period 1946–2023.

Several limitations require stating at the outset. The corpus covers only the UNGA General Debate; positions articulated in closed Security Council sessions, bilateral correspondence, or domestic political discourse fall outside the scope of analysis. The speeches examined represent a specific genre — formal, high-stakes, public address before a global institutional audience — and the legitimation strategies identified here should not be assumed to correspond to the full range of legal or political argumentation available to states in other settings. No claim about the actual legality of the conduct in question is made anywhere in this thesis: the designation "alleged violation" in every case signals an independently documented accusation, not a legal finding. The analysis examines how states respond to that accusation in public discourse, not whether the accusation was legally correct. A further constraint concerns non-English-language speeches: while the UNGDC provides translated versions, strategic ambiguity or omissions in translation may affect the precision of coding for certain passages [VERIFY: confirm translation methodology in UNGDC documentation — Baturo et al. 2017].

The thesis is organized as follows. Chapter 1 reviews the relevant literature across three overlapping fields: theories of legitimacy and legitimation in international relations, the UNGA General Debate as a rhetorical and institutional arena, and existing empirical research on legitimation discourse in multilateral settings. Chapter 2 develops the theoretical framework, moving from Habermas's (1984) typology of social action and validity claims through its IR applications in Risse (2000), Müller (2004), and Bjola (2005), to an operational account of legitimation strategy for the purposes of this study. Chapter 3 describes the research design, corpus selection, sampling logic, and coding framework in detail. Chapter 4 presents the empirical findings: frequency and co-occurrence patterns, comparative case analysis across three dominant strategy types, and a longitudinal account of how the repertoire has shifted across the period 1946–2023. Chapter 5 interprets these findings against the theoretical framework, assesses both hypotheses, and identifies the study's contributions and limitations. The conclusion returns directly to the main research question and states what the analysis has established.

[^1]: В названии работы используется периодизация 1945–2023, поскольку 1945 год означает принятие Устава ООН и институциональное оформление послевоенного международно-правового порядка. Корпус UNGDC охватывает 1946–2023 год — начиная с первой сессии Генеральной Ассамблеи; именно этот диапазон используется во всех эмпирических разделах работы.
