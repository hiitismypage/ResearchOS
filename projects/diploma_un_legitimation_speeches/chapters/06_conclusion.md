# Conclusion

The analysis of legitimation discourse in UNGA General Debate speeches between 1946 and 2023 yields findings that are consistent across all four hypotheses and across the full corpus. State leaders facing allegations of legally or normatively contested conduct do not refuse to legitimize: the complete absence of strategic action as a dominant mode — across 33 case-years, four historical periods, and three geopolitical blocs — indicates that the General Assembly forum imposes a structural demand for normative performance that operates independently of the speaker's strategic intent. Every coded speech in the sample, including those delivered by governments under sustained international criticism, contains sufficient justificatory or normative content to be classified as communicative or normative action. This finding directly answers the main research question: the strategies employed are legitimation strategies — oriented toward constructing shared normative grounds, not toward eliminating the challenge altogether.

Of the strategies identified, counter-terrorism and securitization framing (34.3% of coded segments) and procedural-legal claims (27.5%) together account for nearly two-thirds of the sample. Explicit self-defense invocations under Article 51 of the UN Charter appear in only one coded segment; the characteristic pattern is the construction of necessity arguments through securitization language, with formal legal citation as supporting rather than primary vehicle. The temporal distribution of these strategies is not stable. A statistically significant shift — χ²(6) = 21.06, p = .0018 — marks the transition from Cold War normative dominance, when Charter invocations and sovereignty appeals accounted for 63.6% of classified segments, to post-9/11 communicative dominance, when justificatory framing reached 71.0% of classified content in the 2002–2013 sample. September 2001 functions in this data as a threshold crossing rather than a gradual drift: the counter-terrorism infrastructure that followed created a legitimation resource adopted rapidly across the full spectrum of the UN membership. The Post-2014 period partially reverses this trajectory — normative-legal framing recovers to 25.9% — consistent with the intensification of sovereignty-revisionist argumentation among states operating in an environment of increased normative contestation.

The geopolitical dimension of the analysis produces a more complex picture than either convergence or divergence accounts alone can accommodate. At the level of counter-terrorism rhetoric, convergence is unambiguous: post-2001 adoption of securitization framing is cross-bloc and not significantly differentiated across the three analytical groupings. At the level of legitimation register, however, divergence is equally robust: Western states rely predominantly on communicative justification, while Soviet/Eastern and Non-Aligned states rely more consistently on normative-legal invocations — Charter provisions, treaty authority, the non-interference principle. The Fisher's exact pairwise comparisons confirm the Western bloc's deficit in normative-legal framing at p = .023 relative to the Soviet/Eastern grouping and p = .003 relative to the Non-Aligned Movement. These two patterns — surface convergence in rhetorical resources, structural divergence in legitimation register — operate at different analytical levels and are not contradictory. They reflect the availability of a shared post-2001 vocabulary alongside the persistence of structurally conditioned differences in how states relate to the Charter architecture.

These findings make three contributions to the IR literature. At the empirical level, the analysis provides systematic, corpus-based evidence for patterns that prior scholarship has identified through case analysis — confirming, across 102 coded segments, that communicative action is empirically prevalent in UNGA legitimation discourse and more frequent than normative-legal invocation in every post-Cold War period. At the theoretical level, the results extend Bjola's (2005) account of deliberative legitimacy beyond the two episodes — Kosovo and Iraq — on which it was originally grounded, demonstrating that the forum-level constraint it describes holds across three blocs and seven decades. At the interpretive level, the register differentiation finding — Western states employing quasi-legal justificatory framing while non-Western states invoke formal Charter-grounded norms — complicates received narratives of Western states as primary custodians of the 'rules-based order', pointing instead toward structurally conditioned asymmetries that are observable in the discourse independently of any actor's stated normative commitments.

The broader significance of these findings lies in what they reveal about the durability of the UNGA as a legitimation arena. The General Debate remains the single venue at which every UN member state is obligated to appear and address a universal audience — without veto and without the procedural constraints of the Security Council. As the normative architecture of the postwar order has come under sustained pressure since 2014, the question of how states legitimize legally contested conduct before that audience becomes analytically important precisely because the forum continues to function. The evidence presented here suggests that the communicative demand of the General Assembly has not collapsed under normative fragmentation: even in the most recent period of the corpus, states continue to perform legitimation rather than abandon it. Tracking the strategies through which that performance is constructed, and the conditions under which the repertoire shifts, is a precondition for any systematic account of how international norms are contested, sustained, and reconstructed under pressure.
