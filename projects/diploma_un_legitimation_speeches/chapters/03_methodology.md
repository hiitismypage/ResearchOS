# 3. Methodology

## 3.1. Research Design: Directed Qualitative Content Analysis

The analytical approach adopted in this study is directed qualitative content analysis, a design in which theoretical categories derived from prior literature guide the coding procedure rather than emerging inductively from the data. [SOURCE NEEDED: Hsieh & Shannon, 2005 — directed vs. conventional content analysis] The choice of a directed over an inductive approach reflects the theoretical commitments established in Chapter 2: the coding framework was built from Habermas's typology of social action, Bjola's deliberative legitimacy framework, and the strategy typology introduced in Section 2.3. The categories are theory-driven, not data-driven, which means the analysis is confirmatory in orientation — testing whether theoretically anticipated patterns are present in the corpus — rather than exploratory. This has consequences for what the study can and cannot claim, which are addressed in Section 3.5.

The rationale for this design is directly tied to the gap identified in Chapter 1. Existing research on legitimation in UN settings consists predominantly of case studies — analytically rich accounts of individual episodes that cannot speak to patterns across states, violation types, and periods. What the literature lacks is a horizontal account: which strategies are deployed, with what frequency, in what combinations, and how the distribution has shifted over time. Directed qualitative content analysis, supplemented by limited descriptive quantification of code frequencies and co-occurrence patterns, is the instrument through which that horizontal account becomes possible. The quantitative component does not replace interpretive depth — the comparative analysis in Chapter 4 provides that — but it offers a systematic basis for pattern identification that prior case-study work cannot.

The analytical procedure unfolds in two stages. The first stage is *descriptive mapping*: coding all speeches in the sample against the two analytical dimensions (violation type and legitimation strategy), calculating frequencies and co-occurrence patterns across the sample, and assessing Hypotheses 1 and 2 at the aggregate level. The second stage is *comparative qualitative interpretation*: selecting a set of representative cases from the coded sample and examining in closer detail how specific legitimation strategies are realized in speech — which rhetorical resources are deployed, which legal and normative frameworks are invoked, and how the framing responds to the specific allegation being addressed. The two stages are complementary: the descriptive stage establishes what patterns exist across the sample; the interpretive stage examines why particular configurations appear and what they reveal about the communicative structure of legitimation in each context.

The unit of analysis is the argumentative segment — a sentence or sequence of sentences that constitutes a rhetorically complete justificatory move. [SOURCE NEEDED: Krippendorff — unit of analysis in content analysis] A single speech may contain multiple coded segments, each assigned to a violation type and a strategy type independently. This design allows a single address to be coded as responding to more than one allegation and as deploying more than one legitimation strategy, which is empirically realistic: states defending contested conduct typically assemble composite justifications rather than relying on a single argument.

## 3.2. Corpus and Data: the Harvard UNGDC

The primary data source is the UN General Debate Corpus (UNGDC). The original corpus was compiled from UN official records and published on Harvard Dataverse by Baturo, Dasandi, and Mikhaylov (2017); their release brought together the full texts of speeches delivered during the General Debate of the United Nations General Assembly for sessions spanning 1970 to 2014, making them available as a structured text-as-data resource. The extended version used in this study was developed by Jankin, Baturo, and Dasandi (2025), who expanded coverage backward to the inaugural General Assembly session in 1946 and forward through 2023. As employed in the present study, the corpus encompasses 11,127 speeches from 198 countries across nearly eight decades of General Debate sessions. The study period is framed as beginning in 1945 — the year the UN Charter was signed and the postwar normative architecture came into existence — but the earliest speeches in the UNGDC date from 1946, when the first session of the General Assembly convened. The analytical period therefore begins in 1946 in practice, with 1945 marking the normative baseline against which legitimation strategies are assessed.

Jankin, Baturo, and Dasandi (2025) is a corpus-building contribution: its analytical demonstrations — word frequency trends, topic modeling of Sustainable Development Goals, and predictive modeling of voting outcomes — are illustrative rather than substantive IR arguments. The present study draws on the extended corpus to pursue a different analytical objective: examining how states facing allegations of legally or normatively contested conduct construct legitimation discourse across the full postwar period, and how that discourse has evolved. That research question is made tractable by the corpus, but is not addressed by it.

The corpus offers several properties that make it suitable for the analysis of legitimation discourse at scale. Speeches are available as plain-text files, machine-readable and standardized in format, with consistent metadata on the year of delivery, the country, and the session number. The General Debate format is highly uniform across time: each member state receives a designated speaking slot, delivers an address on topics of its choosing, and addresses a plenary audience that includes the full UN membership. This genre stability reduces interpretive noise when comparing speeches across decades and across actors from different political systems and regions.

The primary interface with the corpus is computational. Python scripts are used for three preparatory tasks: extracting speeches for identified state-years from the full corpus, applying keyword-based filters to flag addresses likely to contain legitimation-relevant content, and generating descriptive statistics on the selected sample (session year, geopolitical bloc classification, and violation type distribution). These scripts do not replace the qualitative reading and coding procedure — every speech in the final sample is read and coded manually — but they reduce the volume of material requiring close attention and ensure that the sample is drawn systematically from the full corpus rather than from a convenient subset. Applied to the 35-case sample, the pre-screening procedure extracted 931 text segments; of these, 136 (14.6%) were flagged as candidates for manual review on the basis of strategy and violation keyword scores exceeding the threshold.

Two limitations of the corpus require acknowledgment. First, speeches originally delivered in languages other than English are represented in the corpus through official UN translations. The translation process introduces the possibility that strategic nuances in the original — specific word choices that activate or deflect legal categories, rhetorical hedges that shift responsibility — are smoothed out or lost. For most analytical purposes this is an acceptable constraint; for the qualitative interpretive stage, it is a limitation that the analysis notes where relevant. Second, as Baturo et al. (2017, p. 3) observe, General Debate speeches are strategically constructed self-presentations: "member states present themselves exclusively in the guise in which they wish to be known." Rather than treating strategic self-presentation as a distortion to be corrected, the present analysis takes it as its primary object — the strategic construction of normative justification is precisely what the coding framework is designed to capture.

## 3.3. Case Identification: Criterion-Based Exhaustive Selection

The analytical sample includes all speeches in the UNGDC that satisfy the study's inclusion criterion: the delivering state faced documented external criticism or allegation of legally or normatively contested conduct at the time of the relevant UNGA session, and the speech itself contains material — direct or indirect — addressing that contested conduct. No predetermined N constrains the sample; the inclusion criterion is applied systematically to the full corpus. The present study identifies 33 state-year cases meeting both conditions, spanning 18 states across all five UN regional groups and all four analytical periods (Cold War: 8 cases; post-Cold War normative expansion: 9; post-9/11 security turn: 8; post-2014 normative fragmentation: 8). The sample is not designed to represent the population of all UNGA speeches, but it is exhaustive within its criterion: no speech meeting both conditions is excluded.

Case identification proceeds in two steps. In the first step, episodes of legally or normatively contested conduct are identified using contextual documentation: UN reporting (General Assembly and Security Council records, special rapporteur reports), NGO documentation (Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch annual reports), and academic secondary literature on specific conflicts and episodes. These sources serve as contextual indicators that a state faced external criticism or allegation at the time of its UNGA address — they do not establish that a legal violation occurred, and the coding framework does not treat them as proof of illegality. The attribution of wrongdoing is not the study's function; it is identifying when public justification is being performed that drives case selection.

In the second step, identified state-years are checked against the UNGDC to confirm that the speech for that country and session is available in the corpus and contains material relevant to the contested episode. Speeches that make no reference — direct or indirect — to the contested conduct are excluded. The resulting sample is characterized along three analytical dimensions, which will be reported in the descriptive overview in Section 4.1:

- *Regional distribution*: the 18 states in the sample span all five UN regional groups — WEOG (USA, GBR, ISR, YUG), EEG (RUS), Asia-Pacific (CHN, IDN, VNM, PRK, MMR), Arab/ME and adjacent (IRQ, SAU, SYR, IRN, TUR, AZE), and Africa (ZAF, ETH) — with no group entirely absent
- *Violation type coverage*: all five violation type categories are represented — use of force accounts for the largest share (16 of 33 cases), followed by IHL violations (8), human rights allegations (5), territorial integrity (3), and treaty non-compliance (1) [VERIFY: confirm breakdown totals to 33 after Cold War case revision]
- *Temporal distribution*: cases are drawn from all four analytical periods, with near-even distribution: Cold War (8), post-Cold War normative expansion (9), post-9/11 security turn (8), post-2014 normative fragmentation (8)

The temporal distribution reflects a substantive analytical choice. The Cold War period (1946–1989) is included to enable comparison with the post-1990 normative expansion, but practical constraints — sparser secondary documentation, smaller UNGA membership, and OCR quality issues in the corpus for early sessions [VERIFY: confirm OCR coverage and quality for pre-1992 speeches] — mean that the sample is weighted toward post-1990 cases. This weighting is acknowledged as a limitation in Section 3.5.

## 3.4. Coding Framework

The coding framework operationalizes the two analytical dimensions introduced in Section 2.3. Each coded segment is assigned a value on Dimension 1 (violation type) and on Dimension 2 (legitimation strategy). In addition, each coded segment is tagged with one of four historical sub-periods: Cold War (1946–1989), post-Cold War normative expansion (1990–2001), post-9/11 security turn (2001–2013), and post-2014 sovereignty-revisionist fragmentation (2014–2023). This third tag enables the temporal comparison that addresses Sub-question 2 without constituting a separate analytical dimension. The full codebook specifies, for each category, a definition, a set of inclusion criteria, examples of prototypical instances, and decision rules for ambiguous or borderline cases [VERIFY: finalize codebook before empirical chapter]. The present section describes the coding procedure; the category definitions are provided in Chapter 2. The framework identifies eight legitimation strategy types — one more than the seven originally specified in the research proposal. The additional category results from the decision in Section 2.3 to split the original *moralization and counter-terrorism framing* category into two analytically distinct types: *moral and humanitarian framing* (category 4) and *counter-terrorism and securitization framing* (category 5), on the grounds that the two involve different rhetorical resources and different relationships to the postwar normative architecture.

Coding proceeds in three phases. In the *preparation phase*, each speech in the sample is read in full to establish context: the political situation facing the state at the time of delivery, the specific allegations or criticisms circulating in the international arena, and the overall rhetorical register of the address. This contextual reading reduces the risk of miscoding segments whose justificatory function is only intelligible against the backdrop of a specific episode. In the *segmentation phase*, the speech is divided into argumentative segments — rhetorically complete justificatory moves — and each segment is provisionally assigned to one or more coding categories. In the *finalization phase*, all provisional codes are reviewed against the codebook definitions and decision rules, and a final code is assigned.

The codebook incorporates explicit decision rules for three recurring sources of ambiguity. First, speeches often contain passages that combine multiple strategies within a single argumentative sequence — for instance, invoking self-defense (category 1) while simultaneously delegitimating the accuser (category 6). Where strategies are analytically distinguishable within the same segment, both are coded; where they are fused into a single argument, the dominant strategy is coded and the secondary one noted. Second, the boundary between *denial and reinterpretation* (category 3) and *sovereignty and non-interference* (category 2) can be narrow: a government may deny that a domestic action constitutes a human rights violation while simultaneously characterizing the allegation as external interference. The decision rule assigns primacy to the dominant rhetorical move — factual challenge versus jurisdictional objection. Third, *historical and existential narratives* (category 8) often function as contextualizing frames for other strategies rather than as independent justifications. Where historical framing accompanies a legal argument without carrying its own justificatory weight, it is coded as secondary.

The coding procedure is applied iteratively. An initial round of coding on a pilot subset (approximately 10–15 speeches, drawn to reflect the diversity criteria above) is used to test the codebook categories and refine decision rules before the full sample is coded. All revisions to the codebook made during the pilot phase are documented in an audit trail.

## 3.5. Reliability, Validity, and Scope of Claims

Content analysis produces interpretable results only if the coding procedure is sufficiently reliable and the analytical categories are sufficiently valid. Both dimensions require explicit treatment.

*Reliability* is addressed through double-coding: a subset of the sample (approximately 20% of speeches) is independently coded by a trained research assistant working from the same codebook. Inter-coder reliability is calculated using Cohen's kappa [SOURCE NEEDED: Cohen's kappa — cite standard reference], with a target threshold of κ ≥ 0.70 regarded as acceptable for this type of categorical coding. [VERIFY: confirm standard threshold for this type of research] Disagreements between coders are resolved through discussion and, where necessary, revision of the codebook's decision rules. All disagreements and resolutions are documented in the audit trail. The audit trail serves a dual function: it supports reliability assessment and it provides a record of the interpretive decisions that shaped the final codes, making the analytical procedure transparent and in principle reproducible.

*Validity* in directed content analysis depends on whether the coding categories adequately capture the phenomenon of interest. Construct validity is grounded in the theoretical derivation of the categories: each of the eight legitimation strategy types maps onto a recognizable pattern in the IR literature on justification and diplomatic discourse. Face validity is assessed through the pilot phase: if trained coders consistently struggle to assign segments to categories, this signals that the category definitions require refinement. External validity — the question of whether findings from the purposive sample generalize beyond it — is addressed by scope-of-claims delimitation rather than by statistical inference.

Claims are appropriately bounded by the study's design. The analysis identifies and describes the distribution of legitimation strategies *within the selected sample* of speeches from states facing allegations of legally contested conduct; it does not claim that this distribution is representative of all UNGA speeches or of all state behavior in the face of external criticism. The temporal dimension — tracing how strategy distributions shift across historical periods — is similarly constrained: no causal mechanisms driving that evolution are established. Most importantly, no adjudicative claims are made: the coding of a speech segment as deploying self-defense framing does not constitute a finding that the state's action was or was not lawful. The object of analysis is justificatory discourse — how states construct public accounts of their conduct — not the legal or factual merits of those accounts.

Two further constraints on the scope of claims are noted. First, the corpus covers only the UNGA General Debate, not Security Council statements, bilateral diplomatic communications, or other forms of diplomatic address. Legitimation strategies operative in other venues may differ from those deployed in the General Debate, and the study cannot speak to those differences. Second, translation from non-English originals introduces the risk of systematic distortion in cases where the specific legal vocabulary of the original is not fully preserved in the UN translation. Where this risk is especially salient — as in the analysis of speeches from non-Anglophone states on legally technical matters — the interpretive analysis flags the constraint explicitly.

Subject to these constraints, the methodology is designed to produce a systematic, transparent, and theoretically grounded account of legitimation strategies in UNGA General Debate addresses — a foundation for the empirical analysis that follows in Chapter 4.
